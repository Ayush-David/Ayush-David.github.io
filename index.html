Research Vision

I build perception systems that have to work in the real world — where the sensor is noisy, the compute budget is fixed, and the benchmark does not exist yet.

  1. From Pattern Matching to Causality

    Most vision models are lookup tables with good interpolation. I want models that hold a belief, update it across frames, and know when they are wrong. Temporal reasoning. Structured uncertainty. Not just "what is in this image" but "what should I expect next, and why."

  2. Physics as a Constraint, Not a Prior

    When you have no high-resolution ground truth — satellite thermal imagery, for example — a standard loss function makes the model hallucinate detail. I bake physical laws directly into the optimization. The Planck equation is not a regularizer; it is a hard constraint the model cannot violate.

  3. Inference Has a Physical Cost

    A model that runs at 200 ms on a GPU means nothing on a Jetson at the edge. I compress perception down to its Kolmogorov minimum: the smallest representation that still closes the loop. Simulation-to-real transfer is part of this — the gap between CARLA and a real road is not a detail.

The common thread: the model has to be right in the field, not just on the test set.
